# Application: [Headspace]

- Most important difference between pipelines: [The automated pipeline produced broad personas, and sometimes requirements that overlapped heavily. When giving examples of reviews for the review groups, at times the automated pipeline gave examples that were not relevant to the group, which affected the personas and requirements down the line. The manual pipeline was the most concise and focused, but it was very time consuming. The hybrid was a nice middle ground. There was a good starting point and with revision, it became more focused and less redundant.]

- Most useful pipeline: [The hybrid pipeline is the most useful. It built on what the automated pipeline started, making it just an overall improved version of it]

- Most surprising finding: [Despite automation, all pipelines achieved a testability rate of 1.0, meaning every requirement could be tested. However, the automated outputs had higher ambiguity (0.6) compared to the manual pipeline (0.5455), showing that grammatically correct requirements do not always guarantee clarity.]

- Observed weakness in the automated pipeline: [Some automated personas included assumptions not fully supported by reviews, and several requirements were too generic, which could make testing less precise. Automated outputs lacked the nuances captured in the manual pipeline]

- According to the metrics, the manual pipeline had the best traceability, test count, requirements count, and best ambiguity ratio. But the manual pipeline wass just too time consuming to be the overall best out of the three. The hybrid pipeline, having only a slighlty worse ambiguity ratio but a slightly better review coverage score, while also saving a lot of time, makes it the better pipeline out of the three.
